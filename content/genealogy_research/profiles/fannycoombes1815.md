## Birth & Family
Frances "Fanny" Marion Coombes was born on the 17th of February, 1815 to parents John Coombs and Frances Maria Coombs (n&eacute;e Browne). Fanny's birth was registered at the parish of Dartfort, Kent.[^1] Fanny was baptised on the 13th of May, 1818 with her sister Lydia under the surname Combe.[^2] Fanny was the second of nine children, she had one older brother and seven younger siblings: John Coombs, born in 1810;[^3] Lydia Coombs, born in 1816;[^4] Charles Wedmore Coombs, born in 1818;[^5] Caroline Eliza Coombs, born in 1820;[^6] Susannah Coombs, born in 1823;[^7] George Coombs, born in 1825[^8] (died in infancy);[^9] Esther Coombs, born in 1826;[^10] and George Coombs, born in 1831.[^11]

## Emigration to Australia

Around the beginning on 1836, the British Government implemented a plan to encourage the emigration of single women to the Australian colony (which was only ~20 % female)[^], by providing free passage for single or widowed women between the ages of 15 and 30 to Tasmania, and free housing upon their return until they found a suitable situation there. The London Emigration Committee placed notices in parishes throughout the United Kindom calling for expression of interest from young women who would  "be likely to condusct themselves creditably and usefully in the colony".[^]

{{< figure library="true" src="FreeEmigration.jpg" title="Advertisement for Free Passage to Australia for Single Women" lightbox="true" >}}

Fanny would have been approximately 21 when she applied for this emigration program. The process was by no means straightforward. After submitting a written aplication accompanied by a "satisfactory Certificate of Character from a Minister resident in the Parish, or from some other respectable Person to whom the Applicant is well-known". Next Fanny, and possibly her parents, would have been interviewed by the London Emigration Committee - a group of about 20 well-respected members of society representing the clergy, church members, merchants, bankers and other well-respected members of their communities.

Frances Maria would have been interviewed by the LEC, a group of some 20 respected members of society representing the clergy, church members, merchants, bankers and other well-respected members of their communities. Perhaps accompanied by her parents John and Frances the young Frances Maria presented herself along with the two required letters of recommendation as to her state of health and good character. Finally, a personal interview, to be conducted by one of the committee members, would take place before the final acceptance papers would be issued. 

Under this scheme 14 ships sailed from England to Australia in the period from 1833 to 1837 involving 3000 women in this first wave of female emigration. The women were required to be single or widowed, able bodied and prepared to leave the home of their birth. On board ship they were accompanied by a surgeon and often his wife or mature women known as matrons, selected for their ability to attend to the health and welfare of female passengers. The women were assured of care and protection by the Local Government upon arrival until suitable employment was arranged and agreed between the emigrant and the employer.

With her application accepted, passage paid for by the Government at cost of approximately £12, Frances Maria was selected to sail on board the AMELIA THOMPSON on 25th April 1836 from Gravesend.
